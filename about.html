Ukrainian-Russian Meme Wars

What is the goal of the project?

The goal of the project is to gain a better understanding of the memetic discourse around the 2022 Russian-Ukrainian War and creating a classification system to identify memetic trends. This project focuses mainly on English-language memes from a few large producers of memes, which reveals just one area of the memetic landscape, but one can expect that these are the memes that the average American or English-speaking meme enthusiast might come across. Memes from Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, and Reddit offer a very different take on the war than editorial, government, or academic actors, as memers feel more licensed to poke at the extreme, obscene, and absurd. Memes do not offer a more "true" or valid look at the situation than a conventional source, but they often offer an unconstrained perspective which traditional media lack. Memes, as a part of folklore, serve a number of purposes from entertainment to education to emotional relief. We can only guess at the intentions behind these memes, but we can infer their meaning through their content and context. The majority of memes here invoke sympathy and support towards the Ukrainian cause, testament to the fact that the Western world is largely against the war. Perusal of the Russian-speaking web likely paints a very different picture of the conflict. Therefore, the project is a window into the thoughts, feelings, and whimsies of English-language memers during the first 70 days of the 2022 Russian-Ukrainian war. The memes present criticsm, commentary, humor, and foolishness in varying amounts. I do not condone the messages or images in the memes, and in fact, many of them are disturbing given the humanitarian catastrophe of the war. My heart goes out to everyone affected by the war, and I hope this project draws more attention towards the absurdity and suffering of war and the importance of devoting resources towards ending this war.

Where are the memes from?

The memes are collected from four social media sites: Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, and Reddit.

Twitter

  • @Ukraine: The official Twitter account of Ukraine. This account is obviously pro-Ukraine and is particularly interesting because of its official status on Twitter. Although this account is more formal than accounts like @MENAConflicted, it is less formal than an official Russian government twitter like @mfa_russia (the Russian Ministry of Defense Twitter)
  • @MENAConflicted: Also known as "Ukraine Memes for NATO Teens", this Twitter account has almost 40 thousand followers. It is unclear who runs this account, but they were the top result on Twitter for "memes about the Russia-Ukraine war". The account posts multiple times a day: usually humorous memes but also military updates and footage. This account would be an example of an 'informal' memetic actor.
  • @USEmbassyKyiv: I have only included one tweet from @USEmbassyKyiv (the one comparing Kyiv and Moscow over time). This meme seemed to evoke @Ukraine's tone, but it is not typical for the @USEmbassyKyiv Twitter account, which mostly posts news updates. It is interesting that @Ukraine is making use of memes while other government Twitters largely stay away from them.

Facebook

I collected memes from a few popular and slightly less popular Ukraine-Russia Facebook meme groups, including:

  • Memes if Russia invade Ukraine (4.2k members)
  • Russian vs Ukraine memes (~800 members)
  • MemeForUkraine (~900 followers)
  • @russianmemesjustforfun (8k followers)
  • Ukraine-Russian memes (~450 members)

Instagram

A search on Instagram for "ukraine russia memes" provided the following accounts which I used for collection:

  • @ukrainian_meme_force (3k followers)
  • @ukrainearmedmemes (2.5k followers)

Reddit

All Reddit memes are from r/memes, a subreddit with 18.7 million members.

About the collection process

How did I choose the particular memes?

I chose mainly English-language memes from popular social media sites such as Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, and Reddit. This project is a first look at the meme space, so I wanted to search several sites and cover a "representative" sample of English-language memes, since each source has its own audience and pecularities. To narrow my search, I excluded generic gifs, images and memes that have been repurposed in the war context because I am not interested in general meme use and commentary during wartime but rather new memes which have incorporated text and images specific to the Russia-Ukraine war. I have attempted to collect memes from after February 24th, 2022 for relevancy. Choosing English-language memes from February 2022 - May 2022 paints a picture of the memetic landscape at a certain point in time for a particular audience. When there was an abundance of memes to choose from, I chose memes with the most upvotes, retweets, likes, comments, or shares. I was also particularly interested in memes which offered narratives or criticism that ran against mainstream discussions of the war.

What methods did I use to collect the memes?

Twitter: I scraped Twitter with Tweepy, a Python library to access the Twitter API. Code is here.

Facebook, Instagram, Reddit: I collected memes from Facebook, Instagram, and Reddit manually by searching for keywords relating to the Russia-Ukraine war and saving images as I went.

Why didn't I include timestamps or links back to the original posts?

Collecting this much information is outside the scope of my current project, which aims to provide a classification on the general meme landscape of the Russia-Ukraine 2022 war in the English-language social media sphere. In my case, I felt like the original post was not critically important because I saw the same meme posted in several places. Future projects could gather timestamps or links back to the original posts to investigate the timeline of meme evolution, public responses to memes, and more specific questions of audience and interaction.
