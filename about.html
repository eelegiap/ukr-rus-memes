Ukrainian-Russian Meme Wars

What is the goal of the project?

The goal of the project is to gain a better understanding of the memetic discourse around the 2022 Russian-Ukrainian War and creating a classification system to identify memetic trends. This project focuses mainly on English-language memes from a few large producers of memes, which reveals just one area of the memetic landscape, but one can expect that these are the memes that the average American or English-speaking meme enthusiast might come across. Memes from Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, and Reddit offer a very different take on the war than editorial, government, or academic actors, as memers feel more licensed to poke at the extreme, obscene, and absurd. Memes do not offer a more "true" or valid look at the situation than a conventional source, but they often offer an unconstrained perspective which traditional media lack. Memes, as a part of folklore, serve a number of purposes from entertainment to education to emotional relief. We can only guess at the intentions behind these memes, but we can infer their meaning through their content and context. The majority of memes here invoke sympathy and support towards the Ukrainian cause, testament to the fact that the Western world is largely against the war. Perusal of the Russian-speaking web likely paints a very different picture of the conflict. Therefore, the project is a window into the thoughts, feelings, and whimsies of English-language memers during the first 70 days of the 2022 Russian-Ukrainian war. The memes present criticsm, commentary, humor, and foolishness in varying amounts. I do not condone the messages or images in the memes, and in fact, many of them are disturbing given the humanitarian catastrophe of the war. My heart goes out to everyone affected by the war, and I hope this project draws more attention towards the absurdity and suffering of war and the importance of devoting resources towards ending this war.

Where are the memes from?

The memes are collected from four social media sites: Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, and Reddit.

Twitter

  • @Ukraine: The official Twitter account of Ukraine. This account is obviously pro-Ukraine and is particularly interesting because of its official status on Twitter. Although this account is more formal than accounts like @MENAConflicted, it is less formal than an official Russian government twitter like @mfa_russia (the Russian Ministry of Defense Twitter)
  • @MENAConflicted: Also known as "Ukraine Memes for NATO Teens", this Twitter account has almost 40 thousand followers. It is unclear who runs this account, but they were the top result on Twitter for "memes about the Russia-Ukraine war". The account posts multiple times a day: usually humorous memes but also military updates and footage. This account would be an example of an 'informal' memetic actor.
  • @USEmbassyKyiv: I have only included one tweet from @USEmbassyKyiv (the one comparing Kyiv and Moscow over time). This meme seemed to evoke @Ukraine's tone, but it is not typical for the @USEmbassyKyiv Twitter account, which mostly posts news updates. It is interesting that @Ukraine is making use of memes while other government Twitters largely stay away from them.

Facebook

I collected memes from a few popular and slightly less popular Ukraine-Russia Facebook meme groups, including:

  • Memes if Russia invade Ukraine (4.2k members)
  • Russian vs Ukraine memes (~800 members)
  • MemeForUkraine (~900 followers)
  • @russianmemesjustforfun (8k followers)
  • Ukraine-Russian memes (~450 members)

Instagram

A search on Instagram for "ukraine russia memes" provided the following accounts which I used for collection:

  • @ukrainian_meme_force (3k followers)
  • @ukrainearmedmemes (2.5k followers)

Reddit

All Reddit memes are from r/memes, a subreddit with 18.7 million members.

About the collection process

How did I choose the particular memes?

I chose mainly English-language memes from popular social media sites such as Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, and Reddit. This project is a first look at the meme space, so I wanted to search several sites and cover a "representative" sample of English-language memes, since each source has its own audience and pecularities. To narrow my search, I excluded generic gifs, images and memes that have been repurposed in the war context because I am not interested in general meme use and commentary during wartime but rather new memes which have incorporated text and images specific to the Russia-Ukraine war. I have attempted to collect memes from after February 24th, 2022 for relevancy. Choosing English-language memes from February 2022 - May 2022 paints a picture of the memetic landscape at a certain point in time for a particular audience. When there was an abundance of memes to choose from, I chose memes with the most upvotes, retweets, likes, comments, or shares. I was also particularly interested in memes which offered narratives or criticism that ran against mainstream discussions of the war.

What methods did I use to collect the memes?

Twitter: I scraped Twitter with Tweepy, a Python library to access the Twitter API. Code is here.

Facebook, Instagram, Reddit: I collected memes from Facebook, Instagram, and Reddit manually by searching for keywords relating to the Russia-Ukraine war and saving images as I went.

Why didn't I include timestamps or links back to the original posts?

Collecting this much information is outside the scope of my current project, which aims to provide a classification on the general meme landscape of the Russia-Ukraine 2022 war in the English-language social media sphere. In my case, I felt like the original post was not critically important because I saw the same meme posted in several places. Future projects could gather timestamps or links back to the original posts to investigate the timeline of meme evolution, public responses to memes, and more specific questions of audience and interaction.

About the classification system

Format

  • 2-panel: Memes with 2 panels for images or text. This format often sets up a dichotymy between two halves of the meme or sets up a a short narrative where the second half is a turn of phrase or events.
  • Hand-drawn: Hand-drawn memes might invoke traditional propaganda artwork or feature older hand-drawn images repurposed into new settings.
  • Image macro: An image macro is a broad term used to describe captioned images that typically consist of a picture and a witty message or a catchphrase. They are extremely common in this dataset and these are examples: "Germany WYD", "Used to This", and "Teens vs. Putin".
  • Multi-panel: Memes with 3 or more panels with images or text. Because these memes feature more sections, there is usually some kind of storyboard set-up with a relatively longer narrative.
  • Unconventional: Memes that don't fit into the traditional image macro format. In this dataset, we have a bingo card, a password checker, a Venn diagram, and others.

Genre

  • Dank: The term "dank" is often used to describe a meme in which the comedy is excessively overdone and nonsensical, to the point of being comically ironic (source).
  • Geopolitics: The geopolitical memes in this dataset often relate to national and international actors and their interactions with respect to the war. Common actors are the Russian government, European and Middle Eastern countries, and international bodies like NATO.
  • Meta-commentary: The meta-commentary memes often talk about talking about the war. It is a reflection on the commentary that has been put forth in different circles about the war, sometimes with respect to (slack)activism or American exceptionalism.
  • Military: The military memes are pretty straightforward. These memes generally uplift the Ukrainian military and put down the Russian one, although there are memes critical of different military actors.
  • Pop culture: Memes invoking pop culture are extremely common and run the gamut from TV shows to movies to celebrities.
  • Ukrainian folk culture: Stereotypical or idealized aspects of Ukrainian folk culture such as farming, tractors, babushkas find their way into the war memes.

Template

Please see https://knowyourmeme.com/ for more info on all of the different meme templates!

Stance

  • Criticism of Slacktivism: Slacktivism is the practice of supporting a political or social cause by means such as social media or online petitions, characterized as involving very little effort or commitment. These memes make fun of people who feel like they're helping the cause by performing small gestures on social media.
  • Denigrating Russia & Putin: Many memes make fun of or bash Russia and Vladimir Putin personally. Sometimes this is done by contrasting with Ukraine and Zelensky.
  • Military ridicule and boasting: These memes will either boast about Ukraine's military prowess and wins, while making fun of and denigrating the Russian military. The ridicule will go so far as to make fun of Putin for threatening the use of nuclear weapons.
  • Political & Historical Criticism (other): These memes levy political and historical criticism which is not explicitly pro-Ukraine. Some memes may be critical of Ukraine, critical of other military/govt actors, or unclear about their intention.
  • Political & Historical Criticism (pro-Ukraine): These memes levy political and historical criticism which is explicitly pro-Ukraine or anti-Russia.
  • Relatable takes: There are some more relatable memes which either "bring the war home" or focus on people's personal attachments to the war.
  • Ukrainian pride: This category can overlap with military ridicule and boasting and other pro-Ukraine posts. Some of the memes celebrate Ukraine as the underdog, Ukrainian culture, and Ukrainian military success.
  • Zelensky > Putin: Many memes contrast the two leaders, often in unflattering ways towards Putin and in admiring ways towards Zelensky. The Chad vs. Virgin dichotymy is an classic take.

Keywords

Recurrent people, places, and things that appear in the memes are: Azov, Covid, Crimea, EU/NATO/UN, Farming, Finland, Flags, Germany, Ghost of Kyiv, History, Hitler, Mariupol, Moska, Nuclear weapons, Poland, Putin, Sanctions, Snake Island / Russian Warship, UK, USA, USSR, WWIII, Women, and Zelensky.

Of particular interest are the flag memes (memes with superimposed flags), Ghost of Kyiv, the Germany memes, farming memes, memes that reference historical events, and memes referencing the Snake Island / Russian Warship incident.

Findings from the project

The project has unearthed a number of different themes and patterns in recent English-language memes involving the 2022 Russian-Ukrainian War. Articles have already been written about the Ukrainian government Twitter account and its relatively novel use of memes during wartime, but it was interesting to aggregate their memes and contrast them with memes from different sources. I was surprised by the historical awareness and knowledge shown in many of the memes, which showed that the creator had both an understanding of the historical context and a strong grasp on how the images and text in memes evoked very specific feelings. In general, the archive shows a commitment to unrestrained ridicule, commentary, and criticsm. The frequent use of humor throughout the memes raises questions about applications of humor during wartime. Some memes are genuinely pretty funny while others resonate quite darkly and leave the viewer with an uncomfortable feeling. The humor, of course, depends on who's looking at the meme. The understandability of the memes varies across the archive. As a "digital native", I feel like I can explain the majority of the memes, but some are still indecipherable to me. Memes on the internet often go through decontextualization out of their original context, but I have further decontextualized the memes by taking them out of their original platform, rendering the memes even more cryptic. This collage of images and text paints a picture of the English-language internet response to the war, showing overwhelming support for the Ukrainian people and military. This support for Ukraine manifests through military boasting of Ukraine's wins and ridicule of Putin's losses, disparagement of Putin on professional and personal levels, historical commentary and comparisons, and more. Unfortuantely, the reality is that Ukraine's military is much smaller than Russia's. However, the internet response to the war has been largely pro-Ukraine while remaining nuanced, nuanced not in its decorum or civility but in its unrelenting examination of the situation from many sides.

By Paige Lee (May 2022, FM150 Internet Folklore at Harvard University)
